Status: draft for legal and scientific review  
Last updated: 2026-07-11

## Subject

Submission to the renewed Australian Animal Welfare Strategy: national phase-out of 1080 by 2030

## Draft submission

Bite Back welcomes the renewal of the Australian Animal Welfare Strategy and the proposed Animals in the wild chapter. The renewal is an opportunity to set a national direction for animal management that is effective, evidence-based and aligned with contemporary welfare expectations.

We ask that the renewed AAWS include a clear national commitment to phase out and permanently prohibit sodium fluoroacetate, or 1080, for vertebrate animal control no later than 31 December 2030.

We recognise that invasive-animal management can be necessary to protect native species, ecosystems, livestock and rural livelihoods. A 1080 phase-out must not mean abandoning those responsibilities. It should mean replacing 1080 immediately where lower-welfare-impact methods can effectively achieve the management objective, and funding transition work where replacement is not yet straightforward.

## Proposed AAWS wording

Australian governments will eliminate the use of sodium fluoroacetate, or 1080, for vertebrate animal control no later than 31 December 2030. From adoption of the Strategy, jurisdictions will require demonstrably lower-welfare-impact methods wherever they can effectively achieve the stated management objective, while funding species-specific replacement pathways for remaining uses.

## Implementation commitments requested

The renewed AAWS should include:

1. A national phase-out timetable ending routine 1080 use by 31 December 2030.
2. Immediate replacement of 1080 where lower-impact registered or otherwise lawful methods can effectively achieve the objective.
3. A Commonwealth, state and territory transition fund for remote, aerial, landscape-scale and other unresolved uses.
4. Annual public reporting of product quantities, delivery method, target species, program objective, measured outcome and non-target incidents.
5. Necessity, efficacy and welfare assessment before each remaining 1080 program during the transition.
6. Independent review of transition exemptions.
7. APVMA and state/territory implementation work to amend product approvals, permits, authorisations and operating procedures before the deadline.

## Rationale

The current national framework leaves 1080 use fragmented across product approvals, state and territory controls, land-management programs and agency procedures. This fragmentation makes it difficult for the public to see where 1080 is used, why it is used, whether it achieves the stated objective, and what non-target harms occur.

RSPCA Australia has concluded that available evidence does not support describing 1080 as humane. Agriculture Victoria's recent review found lower relative welfare impacts for PAPP in foxes and wild dogs, sodium nitrite in feral pigs, and properly performed shooting where feasible. The same review also identifies operational, cost and evidence limitations that must be dealt with through planning and funding.

That evidence supports a managed transition rather than indefinite reliance on 1080.

## Suggested AAWS milestones

2027:

- AAWS adopted with phase-out commitment.
- National 1080 replacement working group established.
- Annual reporting template agreed.
- Jurisdictions identify uses where lower-impact replacement can begin immediately.

2028:

- First public reporting year.
- Replacement pilots funded for remote and aerial uses.
- Remaining-use necessity assessments published.

2029:

- APVMA and jurisdiction implementation changes drafted.
- Transition exemptions narrowed and independently reviewed.
- Procurement shifts away from routine 1080 reliance.

2030:

- Routine use ends.
- Final prohibition instruments take effect by 31 December 2030.
- Any residual emergency exemption is public, time-limited and independently reviewed.

## Questions for government

1. Will the renewed AAWS include a measurable deadline for reducing and ending 1080 use?
2. What lower-impact methods does each jurisdiction already accept as suitable for foxes, wild dogs and feral pigs?
3. Which uses are considered unresolved, and what evidence or infrastructure is missing?
4. What data will governments publish annually about 1080 use and incidents?
5. How will governments ensure that transition funding supports conservation, livestock protection and animal welfare together?
