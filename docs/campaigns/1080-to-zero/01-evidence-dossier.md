# 1080 to Zero - evidence dossier

Status: working evidence brief  
Last updated: 2026-07-11

## Bottom line

1080 is a legal, restricted poison still used in Australia for vertebrate pest-animal control. Governments argue that it protects native species, ecosystems, agriculture and livestock. Those objectives are real. The campaign position is that Australia should meet them through lower-impact methods wherever possible and funded transition work where replacement is not yet straightforward.

## What 1080 is

1080 is sodium fluoroacetate. In Australia it is used in bait products for pest-animal control, including uses directed at foxes, wild dogs, rabbits and feral pigs. APVMA regulates agricultural and veterinary chemical products up to retail supply, while state and territory governments control use after supply through local laws, permits, directions and operating rules.

Sources:

- APVMA 1080 overview: https://www.apvma.gov.au/resources/frequently-searched-chemicals/1080
- APVMA chemical-review listing: https://www.apvma.gov.au/chemicals-and-products/chemical-review/listing/1080

## Welfare evidence

RSPCA Australia states that the available evidence does not support describing 1080 as humane. Reported signs can include vomiting, disorientation, frantic behaviour, vocalisation, muscle spasms and convulsions. The campaign should treat this as the central welfare concern, while avoiding claims about every animal or every use that cannot be substantiated.

Source:

- RSPCA Australia Knowledgebase: https://kb.rspca.org.au/knowledge-base/what-is-the-rspcas-view-on-using-1080-for-pest-animal-control/

## Alternatives and transition evidence

Agriculture Victoria's review of 1080 and other pest animal control methods is the most useful government source for a balanced campaign argument. It supports three campaign points:

- PAPP can have a lower relative welfare impact than 1080 for foxes and wild dogs.
- Sodium nitrite can have a lower relative welfare impact than 1080 for feral pigs.
- Properly performed shooting can have lower individual welfare impact in situations where it is feasible.

The same review also supports caution:

- 1080 may be effective and cost-efficient in some current programs.
- Replacement evidence is not equally strong across all species and operational contexts.
- Aerial and remote programs need specific transition planning.
- Lower-impact methods still require non-target risk controls and welfare review.

Source:

- Agriculture Victoria review: https://agriculture.vic.gov.au/farm-management/chemicals/requirements-for-using-1080-and-PAPP-animal-bait/review-of-the-use-of-1080-and-other-pest-animal-control-methods

## Species and method matrix

| Target or context | Current campaign position | Replacement pathway |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Foxes | Immediate replacement should be required where PAPP or other lower-impact methods can achieve the objective. | PAPP, targeted shooting, exclusion, integrated management and monitoring. |
| Wild dogs and dingoes managed as wild dogs | Immediate replacement should be required where PAPP or non-lethal/integrated methods are operationally suitable. | PAPP, guardian animals, exclusion, targeted shooting, husbandry changes, local conflict prevention and monitoring. |
| Feral pigs | Sodium nitrite should be assessed and used where it can achieve the objective with lower welfare impact and acceptable non-target safeguards. | HOGGONE/sodium nitrite, trapping, targeted shooting, exclusion and integrated control. |
| Rabbits | Replacement is harder and should be funded as a named transition problem. | Biological controls, warren destruction, exclusion, habitat protection and integrated programs, with welfare assessment. |
| Remote or aerial programs | Do not promise immediate universal replacement. Require necessity assessment, public reporting and funded transition work. | Species-specific pilots, procurement, field trials, non-target safeguards and independent welfare review. |
| Public-land programs | Require annual reporting of objective, quantity, location class, target species, measured outcome and non-target incidents. | Public reporting and independent assessment before continued use. |

## What must be reported

Public reporting should include:

- Product and quantity used.
- Jurisdiction and broad location class.
- Target species.
- Delivery method, including aerial or ground baiting.
- Program objective.
- Outcome measurement.
- Non-target incidents.
- Companion-animal incidents where reported.
- Necessity and alternatives assessment.

The campaign should not demand publication of sensitive exact bait locations while baits are active. Public data can use delayed release, spatial aggregation and safety redaction.

## Evidence claims that are safe to use

Use these claims only with source links:

- 1080 remains a regulated poison product in Australia.
- APVMA regulates products up to retail supply, while states and territories control subsequent use.
- The renewed AAWS can set national direction but cannot alone amend every product approval or state use rule.
- RSPCA Australia does not support describing 1080 as humane.
- Government evidence identifies lower-welfare-impact options for several uses, including PAPP for foxes and wild dogs and sodium nitrite for feral pigs.
- Some replacement gaps remain and require funded transition.

## Evidence gaps to investigate

- Current annual quantities of 1080 used by jurisdiction and agency.
- Public-land baiting program maps at safe spatial resolution.
- Non-target and companion-animal incident data.
- Current use of 1080 against native animals in limited or exceptional settings.
- Comparative cost and effectiveness data for lower-impact methods by species and region.
- Availability and cost constraints for PAPP and sodium nitrite.
- Indigenous-led and local landholder views on humane invasive-animal transition.
